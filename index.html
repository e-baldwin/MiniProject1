Good Visualization #3

The audience of this visualization are readers of The Wall Street Journal, most likely in particular sports or football fans. The message that this visualization conveys is the intensity of the sport resulting in many injuries throughout the course of the NFL season. The data that is encoded in this visualization is the cumulative amount of injuries sustained by NFL athletes and the corresponding body group affected. The visualization encodes the data using varying sizes of circles corresponding to the frequency of injuries sustained. These data circles also have values, and this mark is positioned along various sections of an image of a football player’s body to indicate the respective injury. Additionally, some body group injuries are broken down into smaller subsections detailing the type of injury. Readers can identify extremes and see that although the majority of injuries sustained are in the lower body, it is also worrisome to see that a vast proportion of head injuries suffered have been concussions.

  • Truthful - the data is collected throughout the NFL season
  • Functional - effectively communicates the data
  • Beautiful - the visualization uses the full range of the image of a football player’s body in motion
  • Insightful - provides an insight on the breakdown of NFL injuries
  • Enlightening - Provides concerning information for the reader

As a result, this visualization is effective because it conveys data in a way that makes readers aware of the repercussions the NFL season has on the bodies of athletes.

Bad Visualization #3

The audience of this visualization are Alabama government officials and citizens perusing the website for labor statistics. This visualization aims to show a relative comparison of unemployment insurance claims. The data that is encoded in this visualization is the number of unemployment insurance claims per week. The visualization encodes the data using circles, numbers, and colors. Readers can see the amount of claims relative to other weeks.

  • Truthful - the data is collected throughout the Alabama Department of Labor season
  • Functional - hard to decipher because of the random circles scattered around
  • Beautiful - the colored circles are interesting, and there is interaction
  • Insightful - not really, because it merely presents statistics as opposed to indicating why
  • Enlightening - not really, because it doesn’t convey any information or message past the raw data values

This visualization is bad because it does not fulfill the five principles. The data presented is not particularly useful because it is just number values, and the data is encoded using circles, which once again, only presents numbers.

Redesign #3

  1. For redesign 1 I chose a line graph because this can accurately display any noticeable trends along a linear scale. Additionally the date being on the x-axis allows for a linear progression.
  2. For redesign 2 I chose a bar graph because it can accurately compare the data values across weeks, while being categorized within the greater month label. The x-axis allows the data to be partitioned into months, which helps with a forward progression.
  3. For redesign 3 I chose a stacked bar graph because it can compare the data within each month. The colors help to differentiate different weeks within a month, as opposed to the original visualization where the colors were simply corresponding with a random week. However, the stacked bar chart may have problems in that some months have a different amount of weeks.
